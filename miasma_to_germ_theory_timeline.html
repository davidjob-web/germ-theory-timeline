Miasma foundations  ·  Antiquity – 1540s
🏛️
c. 400 BCE
Hippocrates
On Airs, Waters, and Places
Origin of miasma theory

"Bad air" rising from swamps and rotting matter causes illness. A logical explanation for a real pattern — but wrong about the mechanism. This doctrine would dominate medicine for over 2,000 years.

  • People near marshes genuinely were sicker more often — we now know this was due to mosquito-borne malaria, not air. Hippocrates identified a real pattern and explained it the only way available at the time.
  • Without microscopes or germ theory, disease had to be explained through observable phenomena: smell, decay, and environmental change.
  • The theory was internally consistent and had predictive value — telling people to avoid swamps did reduce disease, even if for the wrong reason.
📜
c. 160–210 CE
Galen of Pergamon
Various medical treatises
Miasma codified as doctrine

Systematised Hippocratic miasma into canonical medical doctrine. Endorsed by the Catholic Church and enshrined in European universities for over a millennium — challenging Galen was professionally dangerous.

  • Galenic medicine became the unquestionable authority in European medicine through the Middle Ages and Renaissance.
  • The institutionalisation of his theory made intellectual dissent very costly — later reformers like Semmelweis paid a heavy personal price.
  • Understanding why miasma persisted requires understanding the power of institutions: the Church, universities, and medical guilds all had interests in maintaining established doctrine.
Transitional evidence  ·  1546 – 1855
📖
1546
Girolamo Fracastoro
De Contagione et Contagiosis Morbis
First contagion theory

Proposed that disease spreads via tiny transferable "seeds of disease" (seminaria morbi) — by touch, contaminated objects (fomites), and at a distance. A conceptual precursor to germ theory, 338 years too early to prove.

  • No microscope existed to prove the "seeds" were real — the first microscope capable of revealing microorganisms was still 130 years away.
  • His ideas were largely ignored because miasma better fitted everyday observations.
  • Fracastoro coined the word fomites — objects that can carry infection — a term still in use today in infection control.
  • His work demonstrates that the right idea, without the right technology or institutional support, cannot become accepted science.
🔬
1676 – 1683
Antonie van Leeuwenhoek
Letters to the Royal Society of London
Microorganisms discovered

First person to observe living microorganisms ("animalcules") using self-crafted lenses achieving ~270× magnification. Proved a microscopic living world exists — the essential prerequisite for all germ theory that followed.

  • A cloth draper by trade, he ground his own lenses — a craft technology that gave him microscopes far superior to any available commercially.
  • The Royal Society initially doubted his claims. Robert Hooke independently replicated his findings, providing the verification that gained acceptance.
  • He did not link microorganisms to disease — that step required another 200 years and further technological development.
  • His work was the indispensable technological foundation: without knowing microbes existed, no one could blame them for disease.
1835
Agostino Bassi
Del mal del segno (On Muscardine Disease)
First experimental proof: microbe causes disease

Proved for the first time that a microorganism — a parasitic fungus — causes a specific disease in silkworms. The first experimental demonstration that a living agent causes disease, anticipating Koch's Postulates by 49 years.

  • Used compound microscopes — a direct technological debt to Leeuwenhoek's foundational lens work 160 years earlier.
  • The Italian silk industry was economically critical; silkworm disease caused major losses, providing funding urgency that purely academic research could not command.
  • Pasteur directly acknowledged Bassi's work when he later investigated pébrine disease in silkworms in the 1860s.
  • His experimental logic — isolate the organism, demonstrate causation, re-isolate — foreshadowed Koch's formal four-step methodology.
1847 – 1861
Ignaz Semmelweis
Clinical data, Vienna (1847) · Die Ätiologie... (1861)
Statistical proof of transmission — rejected

Showed that childbed fever mortality fell from ~18% to under 2% when doctors washed hands before deliveries. Disease was transmitted on hands — not air. The evidence was airtight. The medical establishment rejected it anyway.

  • The natural experiment: Ward 1 (doctors who performed autopsies): ~10–18% mortality. Ward 2 (midwives who did not): ~1–2%. The difference was undeniable — and it pointed to doctors, not air.
  • He lacked the germ theory framework to explain why handwashing worked. He described "cadaverous particles" but could not identify them as microorganisms.
  • Without a mechanism, the establishment dismissed his statistics. Germ theory had not yet been established to make his evidence make sense.
  • He died in a psychiatric institution aged 47, likely from the same type of infection he had spent his career fighting. Pasteur's later work retroactively validated everything Semmelweis had found.
1854 – 1855
John Snow
On the Mode of Communication of Cholera, 2nd ed. (1855)
Epidemiological proof — waterborne disease

Mapped 616 cholera deaths around a single water pump on Broad Street, London. Door-to-door interviews revealed contaminated water — not miasma — as the cause. The founding act of modern epidemiology.

  • Used no microscope — his proof was geographic, statistical, and social. He interviewed survivors and mapped cases house by house across Soho.
  • Key natural experiment: A brewery whose workers drank beer (not pump water) had zero cases. A widow far from Broad Street died — she had pump water delivered because she liked the taste.
  • Convinced the local Board of Governors to remove the pump handle, ending the outbreak — a landmark of evidence-based public health intervention.
  • Built on William Farr's vital statistics system, which provided the mortality data Snow needed to analyse. Snow's work was collaborative, not solo.
  • Koch would not identify Vibrio cholerae until 1883 — Snow proved waterborne transmission 29 years before the bacterium was identified.
Germ theory proven & formalised  ·  1857 – 1884
1857 – 1863
Louis Pasteur
Fermentation papers (1857) · Swan-neck flask experiments (1859–61) · Germ Theory of Fermentation (1863)
Spontaneous generation disproved; germ theory established

Disproved spontaneous generation using swan-neck flasks and proved that microorganisms — not chemical processes — cause fermentation and putrefaction. Extended the logic to disease, providing the mechanistic framework that Semmelweis and Snow had lacked.

  • The swan-neck flask: Broth in a flask with a long S-curved neck stayed sterile indefinitely (air entered but particles could not settle). When the neck was broken, microorganisms grew within days. Elegant, decisive, repeatable.
  • Built on Leeuwenhoek (microbes exist), Bassi (a microbe can cause disease), and Fracastoro's conceptual framework — a direct chain of scientific dependency.
  • Funded partly by the French wine and beer industry to solve spoilage problems — commercial pressure accelerated research that changed the world.
  • Endorsed by the French Academy of Sciences, whose prestige gave his findings international authority and reach.
  • Directly inspired Lister's antiseptic surgery. Pasteur's laboratory science became Lister's bedside practice within four years.
1865 – 1867
Joseph Lister
On the Antiseptic Principle in the Practice of Surgery — The Lancet, 1867
Germ theory applied to surgery

Applied Pasteur's germ theory to surgery: carbolic acid (phenol) sterilised wounds and instruments, cutting post-amputation mortality from ~45% to under 15%. The first clinical proof that killing germs prevents infection.

  • Explicitly credited Pasteur's publications as his theoretical foundation — a rare public acknowledgement showing how science builds on prior work.
  • Carbolic acid was already used industrially to treat sewage in Carlisle. Lister borrowed a civil engineering technology and applied it to medicine.
  • Published in The Lancet for immediate reach across the British medical establishment, though full acceptance still took nearly a decade.
  • Provided the first large-scale clinical evidence bridging laboratory germ theory and real-world patient outcomes — transforming science into practice.
  • Retroactively validated Semmelweis's handwashing findings, which had been dismissed 18 years earlier for lacking exactly this mechanistic explanation.
1876 – 1884
Robert Koch
Anthrax (1876) · Tuberculosis (1882) · Cholera (1883) · Koch's Postulates (1884)
Germ theory formalised with a rigorous methodology

Identified the specific bacteria causing anthrax, tuberculosis, and cholera. Then codified four rules — Koch's Postulates — to prove any microorganism causes any disease. Germ theory was no longer a compelling argument; it was a repeatable scientific methodology.

  • Koch's Four Postulates: (1) The microorganism must be found in all cases. (2) It must be isolated and grown in pure culture. (3) The cultured organism must cause disease in a healthy host. (4) It must be re-isolated from the experimentally diseased host.
  • Technology: Aniline dyes (from the German chemical industry) stained bacteria for microscopic identification. Solid agar culture plates — the agar suggested by Angelina Fanny Hesse, often uncredited — enabled pure bacterial colonies to be grown.
  • Also used photomicrography to document findings, allowing global verification and replication.
  • Worked in direct competitive rivalry with Pasteur — their competition accelerated both men's pace and tightened methodological standards across the field.
  • Koch's 1882 TB announcement was witnessed by Paul Ehrlich, who called it "the greatest scientific event" of his life. Ehrlich went on to found immunology and modern chemotherapy.
  • Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1905. His Postulates remain the logical backbone of infectious disease causation research today.
